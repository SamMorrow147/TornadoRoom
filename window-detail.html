Overview

Your CMU openings are already built into the wall — no cutting needed. The process is: frame the opening with a pressure-treated wood buck, flash and waterproof it, then set and seal the window unit. The biggest risk in a below-grade or near-grade basement application is water infiltration, so flashing and sealant done right is the most critical part.

✓ What You Have

  • CMU openings already sized in the wall
  • North wall opening: ~34½" wide
  • South wall opening: ~34¼" wide
  • No cutting required — openings are built in

⚠ Critical Notes Before You Start

  • Confirm a lintel or bond beam exists above each opening — critical for structural load
  • All lumber touching masonry must be pressure treated (PT) — no exceptions
  • This is a near-grade basement wall — waterproofing is not optional
  • Measure rough opening precisely before ordering windows

⚠ Lumber: What "Pressure Treated" Means Here

Any wood that touches masonry (CMU, concrete, mortar) must be pressure-treated lumber rated for ground contact (UC4B). This is the green-tinted lumber at the lumber yard. Regular untreated lumber will rot within a few years in a basement application. Do not use standard kiln-dried 2× material for the buck.

Also: use stainless steel or hot-dipped galvanized fasteners with PT lumber — regular bright nails/screws will corrode rapidly from the preservative chemicals in the wood.

Step-by-Step Installation

1

Verify the Lintel / Bond Beam

Before anything else, confirm that a lintel or bond beam exists above the opening. This is the horizontal structural element that carries the wall load above. In your CMU walls, it's typically a U-block (bond beam block) filled with grout and rebar. If it's not there, stop — you'll need a mason to install one before any window work.

⚠ For a ~34" opening, the bond beam should be 8" deep minimum and bear at least 8" on solid CMU at each end.

2

Measure the Rough Opening Precisely

Measure the CMU opening at three points: top, middle, and bottom of the width, and left, center, and right of the height. Use the smallest dimension for ordering your window. The rough opening should be the window size + ½" on each side (1" total) for shimming and leveling.

Your openings: North wall ~34½" wide · South wall ~34¼" wide

⚠ Measure the actual opening before ordering — these are initial tape measurements. CMU openings can be slightly out of square.

3

Cut and Assemble the PT Buck Frame

Build a rectangular frame (box) from 2×6 pressure-treated lumber (use 2×8 if your CMU wall is 8" wide). Cut pieces for:

  • Sill (bottom): full width of the opening
  • Head (top): full width of the opening
  • Two jambs (sides): height of opening minus top/bottom buck thickness

Assemble with 3" galvanized structural screws. The frame should slide snugly into the CMU opening with ¼"–½" of play on all sides for adjustment.

4

Apply Sealant Behind the Buck

Run a zigzag bead of ASTM C920 sealant (Sikaflex 1A or OSI Quad) on the back face of all four buck pieces before setting them in the opening. This seals the wood-to-CMU joint from behind — the part you can never access again once the window is installed. Use a ⅜"–½" bead over the irregular block surface.

5

Set, Level, and Anchor the Buck

Set the buck frame into the opening. Check for level on the sill (critical — window won't operate correctly if sill is out of level). Check plumb on both jambs. Shim as needed with composite shims.

Once level and plumb, drill through the buck and into the CMU with a hammer drill and masonry bit. Drive Tapcon screws every 12" around the entire perimeter. Countersink the heads slightly. Minimum 2 screws per side on these opening sizes.

6

Flash the Sill and Jambs

Apply self-adhered flashing tape (Vycor Plus or equivalent) starting at the sill:

  1. Sill first — run it across the full sill, lapping up the jambs 6" each side
  2. Jambs next — run up each side, overlapping the sill flashing
  3. Head last — run across the top, overlapping the jamb flashing

This "shingle lapping" sequence means water always sheds over, not under, the layers. Press all edges firmly — no bubbles or gaps.

✓ Do NOT flash the head before the jambs. Water sheds downward — always lap in that order.

7

Install the Metal Drip Cap at the Head

Before installing the window, slide a metal drip cap (head flashing) behind the siding or exterior wall coating above the head buck. This diverts any water running down the wall away from the window. Aluminum or galvanized steel. Overlap flashing tape by 2".

8

Set and Shim the Window Unit

Set the window into the framed opening. Shim the sill to level, then shim the jambs to plumb. Check that the window opens and closes smoothly — this is your plumb/level check. Most basement windows for this size will be vinyl single-hung or hopper-style (hinged at bottom, opens inward at top — good for basement ventilation).

Drive screws through the window frame into the PT buck per the manufacturer's spec. Do not overtighten — you can warp the frame.

9

Foam the Gap

Fill the gap between the window frame and the PT buck with low-expansion window & door foam (Great Stuff Window & Door, or equivalent). Do NOT use regular Great Stuff or big-gap foam — it expands too aggressively and will bow the window frame and prevent it from opening.

Fill in passes, let cure, trim flush.

10

Exterior Seal

Run a bead of backer rod in any remaining gap between the window flange and the CMU/buck exterior face, then caulk over it with paintable polyurethane exterior caulk. Smooth with a wet finger. This is the final weatherseal.

Also run a bead of sealant at the exterior joint between the buck and the CMU face on all four sides.

11

Interior Finish (Optional at This Stage)

On the interior, you can wrap the window jambs with PT lumber trim, or leave open until interior framing is done. Seal the interior perimeter with a bead of acoustical sealant or paintable caulk for the air seal. This is especially important if the basement will be conditioned space.

⚠ Bond Beam — What It Is, How to Find It, What to Do

Why This Matters for Your Windows

A bond beam (or lintel) above each window opening is what keeps the CMU wall above the opening from collapsing down into it. Without one, the wall loads transfer directly to the window frame — which is not designed to carry that. This is the #1 thing to confirm before any window work begins.

The good news: because your openings were built intentionally into a professionally rebuilt CMU wall, there is a very good chance the bond beam is already there. You just need to identify it visually.

What a Bond Beam Is

A bond beam is a horizontal reinforced concrete element built into a CMU wall using special "U-shaped" or "channel" blocks. The cross-webs of the blocks are knocked out or the blocks are pre-formed with a channel running the full length. Steel rebar is laid inside that channel, then the whole channel is filled with grout (liquid concrete).

The result is essentially a hidden concrete beam running horizontally through the wall — it spans the opening and carries the load of the wall above it to the solid masonry on either side.

Bond Beam vs. Lintel Block — The Difference

Bond beam block: U-shaped channel, open top, can accept both horizontal AND vertical rebar. Most flexible, used throughout the wall height for seismic/structural continuity.

Lintel block: Solid bottom, channel only at top. Used specifically above openings. Cannot accept vertical rebar through it — only horizontal.

Either type works above a window opening. What matters is that one of them is there, filled with grout and rebar.

How to Identify It Visually

✓ Sign 1 — Look at the block course above the opening

Stand back and look at the top of the window opening. The course of blocks directly above it should look slightly different — bond beam or lintel blocks have a slightly lower profile and often have a smoother top face (from being grouted flush). Sometimes you can see grout fill at the ends.

✓ Sign 2 — Tap the wall above the opening

Tap the blocks above the opening with a knuckle or small hammer. A solid, dense sound means grouted (good). A hollow "thud" means empty cores. The course directly above the window opening should sound solid if a bond beam was installed there.

✓ Sign 3 — Check the block ends at the opening

Look at the cut ends of the blocks at the sides (jambs) of the opening. If you can see a cross-section of the block, look for the presence of grout fill and rebar in the horizontal channel. Even partial visibility confirms it.

⚠ What If There Is No Bond Beam?

If you tap the blocks above the opening and they sound hollow, or if you can see empty cores at the jambs, you need a bond beam installed before any window goes in. This is masonry/structural work — options are:

  • Option A — Install a CMU lintel: Remove the course of blocks above the opening, replace with lintel blocks, set rebar, fill with grout, let cure (28 days for full strength, 7 days minimum). Most correct solution.
  • Option B — Steel angle lintel: A structural steel angle (e.g., 3×3×¼" or 4×4×¼" depending on span) can be placed in a saw-cut slot above the opening as a lintel. Faster but requires a masonry saw and exact fit.
  • Option C — Precast concrete lintel: A pre-made concrete lintel dropped into the opening above the window. Available at masonry supply yards sized by span.

⚠ For your ~34" openings: minimum bond beam depth is 8 inches bearing at least 8 inches on solid CMU on each side of the opening. Minimum 2 pieces of #4 rebar at the bottom of the channel.

Field Action — Go Confirm This First

Before purchasing any window materials, go out to the foundation and do the tap test on the block course immediately above each of the two window openings. Then look at the jamb ends of those blocks. Take a photo and share it here — the block profile will make it clear whether a bond beam block was used or if it's a standard CMU.

✓ If your mason built this professionally and intentionally left window openings, there is almost certainly a bond beam already there — this is standard practice. Verify to be sure.

⚠ Do not skip this step. A window installed without a proper lintel above it can fail over time as the wall settles.
